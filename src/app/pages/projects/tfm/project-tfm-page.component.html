Data pipelines

Data collection (datos.gob.es)

We sourced the dataset from Spain’s official open-data portal to ensure data integrity, transparency, and reproducibility for the 2020–2024 study window.

  • Reliability: government source with verifiable provenance.
  • Structured format: CSV files designed for analysis workflows.
  • Regulatory backing: published under the Directorate-General resolution (May 29, 2007), supporting public access and transparency.
  • Granularity: monthly date, CCAA + province, product type, and consumption in metric tons (Tm) to capture seasonality and regional differences.
Initial download: Oct 7, 2024 (through Jul 2024) Update: Dec 26, 2024 (through Oct 2024)

Preprocessing (PySpark + pandas)

We built a repeatable preprocessing pipeline to standardize five CSV extracts and produce analysis-ready tables for EDA, clustering, and forecasting.

  • Load: PySpark CSV ingestion with delimiter (;), header parsing, and schema inference.
  • Text normalization: removed accents/special characters (unicodedata + UDF), uppercased key dimensions (CCAA, province, product), and trimmed/cleaned spacing.
  • Type validation: enforced correct numeric type for Consumo Tm to avoid downstream issues.
  • Consistency checks: ensured no missing values or duplicates after processing.
  • Anomalies & nulls: removed negative consumption values, dropped incomplete rows (na.drop()), standardized monthly date format (YYYY-MM), and reviewed extremes via descriptive stats.
Reproducible Scalable Audit-friendly

Pipeline automation & integration

Automated, modular pipeline

The preprocessing workflow was automated with modular PySpark functions for cleaning, normalization, and validation. This reduces manual effort, increases consistency across yearly extracts, and supports repeatable execution on future datasets.

  • Post-validation: outputs are homogeneous and standardized after cleanup.
  • Operational visibility: integrated logs enable monitoring and faster error detection.
  • Future-ready: designed to ingest updated records and adapt to evolving schemas with minimal rework.
Modular functions Logging Monitoring

Data integration (2020–2024)

Five yearly CSV extracts were consolidated into a single analysis-ready dataset (consumo2020-2024.csv) to enable continuous time-series analysis and cross-year comparisons.

  1. Load yearly CSVs into Spark DataFrames (header=true, inferSchema=true).
  2. Union all years into one DataFrame (union()) and verify a sample output.
  3. Persist as a single CSV file using coalesce(1) and mode="overwrite".
Final dataset: 1.96 MB Ready for EDA, K-Means, SARIMAX

Key findings (2020–2024)

  • Diesel A dominated road fuel demand, exceeding 22M Tm in 2022.
  • Diesel B stayed stable at ~4–5M Tm/year (agriculture and fisheries).
  • Diesel C showed strong seasonality (winter peaks, summer troughs), making it a great SARIMAX case study.
  • Jet fuel collapsed in 2020, recovered to ~6.6M Tm in 2023, then declined in 2024.
  • Unleaded 95 grew until 2024, where a drop suggests changing mobility and efficiency trends.
  • Biodiesel increased from 6,017 → 34,632 Tm (still a small share overall).

Conclusions

K-Means segmentation enables targeted transition strategies

Clustering petroleum consumption by autonomous community proved effective to surface differentiated regional patterns. High similarity groups can benefit from joint policies and shared transition roadmaps.

  • Cluster 0 highlights actionable opportunities: reduce Diesel A dependency via biofuels, improve efficiency in heating and transport, and accelerate renewable infrastructure in rural areas.
  • SARIMAX forecasting complements clustering by testing “what-if” scenarios and estimating demand impact under transition actions.
  • Cluster 2 shows inconsistent correlations (Ceuta/Melilla), signaling the need for deeper local-context analysis before applying broad policies.

SARIMAX limitations: exogenous variables are critical

SARIMAX captured seasonality well, but missing exogenous drivers limited predictive power—especially for highly temperature-dependent fuels such as Diesel C.

  • Without variables like temperature, fuel prices, and policy signals, the model relies on historical patterns and cannot fully explain shocks and structural changes.
  • This reduces accuracy and, more importantly, constrains strategic planning where policy evaluation requires contextual data.
  • A cluster-aware SARIMAX with relevant exogenous inputs would better estimate transition impacts per segment.

Future work

  • Deep-dive analysis of specific clusters, especially Cluster 2, to capture unique local dynamics.
  • Integrate exogenous variables into SARIMAX (temperature, fuel prices, energy policy) to improve realism and accuracy.
  • Explore complementary models (RNNs, hybrid approaches) for richer scenario simulations under volatility regimes.
  • Evaluate energy-policy impact by simulating scenarios (transport electrification incentives, fossil restrictions) per cluster.
  • Extend the study to Europe to benchmark strategies and identify collaboration opportunities for decarbonization.
  • Incorporate new data sources (IoT sensors, satellite signals, market reports) to improve responsiveness and coverage.

Why this matters

In the context of energy transition and public policy, understanding regional fuel demand helps prioritize interventions. Clustering highlights communities with similar behavior, while forecasting estimates how demand could evolve through 2026.

What you can expect

  • Clear regional segments, including a transition-ready cluster (e.g., Castilla-La Mancha, Galicia)
  • Acknowledged edge cases with different dynamics (Cluster 2: Ceuta and Melilla)
  • SARIMAX forecasting with seasonality and a roadmap for adding exogenous variables
